## What to Know Before You Buy

### Indoor vs. Outdoor

This sounds obvious, but the distinction matters more than just weatherproofing. Outdoor cameras deal with bright sunlight, temperature extremes, insects triggering motion alerts, and the need for wider field of view to cover driveways and yards. Indoor cameras can be smaller, cheaper, and optimized for lower-light interior conditions.

Many cameras are rated for both. Look for IP65 or IP67 weather resistance ratings on any camera you plan to use outdoors. IP65 means protected against dust and water jets; IP67 adds temporary submersion protection. Both are suitable for outdoor use in virtually all climates.

### Wired vs. Battery/Wireless

**Wired cameras** connect to power via a USB cable or direct wiring. They never run out of battery, stream continuously if you want them to, and are generally more reliable for 24/7 monitoring. The downside is placement flexibility — you're tied to outlet or junction box locations.

**Battery cameras** go anywhere with no cable runs required. The trade-off: batteries need recharging or replacing every 1–6 months depending on how much motion activity the camera sees. High-traffic areas will drain batteries faster. Most battery cameras also default to event-based recording (saving clips when motion is detected) rather than continuous recording, which conserves battery life.

For front doors and entry points, wired is generally the better long-term choice if you can manage the installation. For monitoring a garage, detached shed, backyard corner, or rental property, battery cameras offer flexibility that's worth the maintenance tradeoff.

### Subscription vs. Local Storage

This is the single most important factor many buyers don't consider until after purchase, and it significantly affects the total cost of ownership over time.

**Subscription-based cloud storage** means your video footage lives on the company's servers, you access it through their app, and you pay monthly (typically $3–$10 per camera or $10–$20 for multiple cameras) to retain more than a day or two of history. If you stop paying, you lose access to recorded footage. Ring Protect, Arlo Secure, Nest Aware — these are all subscription services with ongoing costs.

**Local storage** means footage saves to a microSD card in the camera or a local hard drive (NVR/NAS setup). No monthly fee. No footage disappearing if you cancel a subscription. No third-party company holding your home video.

**The honest math**: A $35 Wyze Cam v3 with a $10 microSD card can store weeks of footage for a one-time $45 investment. A $35 camera with a cloud subscription at $3/month costs $36 extra per year. Over three years, that's $108 in subscriptions on top of the $35 camera purchase.

### Privacy Considerations

Home security cameras create an ongoing stream of footage of you, your family, and your home interior. A few principles worth applying:

Only buy cameras from established companies with clear privacy policies and a track record of handling data responsibly. Avoid off-brand cameras with no documented security practices — there are documented cases of cheap unbranded cameras shipping with remote access vulnerabilities.

If cameras are placed inside the home, know who has access to that footage and under what circumstances the company shares it with law enforcement. Ring has faced criticism for its law enforcement data-sharing practices; this is publicly documented and worth factoring in.

Local storage cameras (Eufy, Wyze with microSD) keep footage on your hardware, off third-party servers, which is a meaningful privacy improvement if this concerns you.

## Indoor vs. Outdoor Placement Guide

**Best indoor placements**: Entry hallways facing front/back doors, living rooms covering main gathering areas, nurseries and children's rooms (with appropriate consent and privacy practices), home offices with valuable equipment.

**Best outdoor placements**: Front door (or use a dedicated doorbell camera), driveway entrance, garage, side gates or backyard access points, ground-floor windows on less-visible sides of the home.

**Placement tips that actually matter**:
- Mount at 8–10 feet height for the best coverage angle while remaining out of easy reach
- Point cameras at entry paths rather than wide open areas to reduce false motion alerts from passing cars and animals
- Avoid pointing cameras directly at bright light sources (streetlights, afternoon sun) — backlighting kills image quality
- For outdoor cameras, point slightly downward (not horizontal) to capture faces as people approach rather than tops of heads

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do security cameras deter crime or just record it?**
Studies suggest visible security cameras do reduce opportunistic crime — most burglars choose targets that appear lower risk, and visible cameras raise perceived risk. The deterrent effect is strongest at entry points (front door, garage) where cameras are visible. Cameras without visible indicators (hidden or small indoor cameras) provide documentation but less deterrence value.

**Q: Do I need professional monitoring with a home security camera?**
No. The cameras in this guide all work as standalone devices — they detect motion, send alerts to your phone, and save footage without any professional monitoring service. Professional monitoring (where a staffed center calls police when alarms trigger) requires a separate security system subscription from companies like ADT or SimpliSafe. Smart cameras replace professional monitoring for many households at a fraction of the cost, though they do require you to personally respond to alerts.

**Q: What happens to my footage if my internet goes down?**
Cameras with local microSD storage (Wyze Cam v3, some Eufy models) continue recording to the card during internet outages. Cloud-only cameras stop saving footage when internet is unavailable. For cameras at primary entry points, local storage provides important continuity — if someone cuts your internet before breaking in, cloud-only cameras may capture nothing while local-storage cameras continue recording.

**Q: How many cameras do I actually need?**
Most homes are well-covered with 3–4 cameras: front door (or doorbell camera), back door or main alternate entry, and one wide-coverage camera covering the driveway or main outdoor area. Larger homes or properties with extensive outdoor areas may need 5–8. Start with entry points first — the majority of break-ins occur through doors, not windows.

**Q: Can I share camera access with family members?**
Yes, all the major platforms support shared access. Ring, Google Home, Eufy, and Arlo all allow you to invite additional users to your camera system through the app. Shared users typically get the same live view and alert access as the primary account holder, though some platforms allow you to restrict specific permissions for shared accounts.
