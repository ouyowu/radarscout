## What to Look for in a Smart Plug

### Voice Assistant Compatibility

Before buying any smart plug, check which voice assistant you use daily. Smart plugs work through cloud connections to your voice assistant's platform — Alexa, Google Assistant, Apple HomeKit, or Samsung SmartThings. Most plugs work with Alexa and Google Home. Apple HomeKit compatibility is much more limited (and typically commands a premium). Samsung SmartThings compatibility is a bonus for users of that platform.

If you have an Echo device and use Alexa primarily: any Alexa-compatible plug will work. If you have Google Nest devices: verify Google Home compatibility. If you're deep in the Apple ecosystem with an iPhone, HomePod, and Apple Watch: Eve Energy is the serious HomeKit option, and Wemo Mini is the budget-friendly middle ground.

### Energy Monitoring

This feature is underrated by first-time buyers and deeply appreciated once you have it. Smart plugs with energy monitoring track real-time watt draw and cumulative kilowatt-hour (kWh) consumption for whatever is plugged in. This answers questions most people have never thought to ask: How much electricity does my old chest freezer in the garage actually use? Is my space heater responsible for my high winter electricity bills? Does my gaming PC use significantly more power when gaming versus idle?

The answer has financial implications. If your utility charges $0.15/kWh and your space heater runs 6 hours/day at 1,500W, that's $0.135/day or roughly $49/month — from a single appliance. Seeing that number in an app often prompts behavioral changes faster than abstract advice about energy conservation.

Energy monitoring adds a modest premium: plugs with this feature typically run $5–$15 more than comparable non-monitoring plugs. For appliances you're curious about, it's worth paying.

### Physical Size and Outlet Blocking

Smart plugs vary significantly in size. Some are compact enough that a two-pack can occupy adjacent outlets of a standard wall plate without either blocking the other. Others are so bulky they block the second outlet entirely. Before buying, check the physical dimensions relative to your outlet configuration.

For power strips and surge protectors, check that the smart plug won't block adjacent outlets there too. Compact rectangular designs generally work better in multi-outlet situations than thick protruding designs.

### App Quality

You'll use the app to set schedules, create automation rules, check energy data, and troubleshoot. A bad app experience significantly degrades the smart plug experience — buggy schedules that reset, confusing automation interfaces, poor notifications, or slow responsiveness all show up in real use.

The Kasa app (TP-Link) and the Eve app (for HomeKit users) are consistently the most praised in the category. Amazon's Alexa app handles the Amazon Smart Plug and is excellent for Alexa ecosystem management. The Wemo app has had historical instability that improved in recent versions. The Wyze app is functional but slower to receive updates.

## Smart Plug Use Cases: What Are People Actually Using These For?

If you're not sure where to start, here are the most popular and useful smart plug applications:

**Table and floor lamps**: The most common use case. A $7–$17 smart plug turns a dumb lamp into a voice-controlled or schedule-controlled light. "Alexa, turn off the living room lamp" at bedtime is more convenient than the switch on most lamp designs.

**Coffee makers**: Set a schedule to start brewing 10 minutes before your alarm. If your coffee maker has a brew-on-startup function (most drip machines will brew when power is restored if you fill them the night before), a smart plug morning schedule handles this automatically.

**Space heaters and fans**: For rooms without smart thermostats, a smart plug lets you voice-control or schedule a space heater or oscillating fan. Note: smart plugs should only be used with resistive heaters (conventional electric heating elements) — not with gas appliances or any appliance whose manual warns against timer use.

**Holiday lighting**: Scheduling outdoor holiday lights to turn on at sunset and off at midnight without any manual intervention is one of the highest-satisfaction uses for seasonal smart plug buyers.

**Standing desks and monitor setups**: Connecting a desk-level power strip to a smart plug lets you control your entire desk setup — monitor, desk lamp, speakers — with a single "office on/office off" voice command or schedule.

**Identifying energy hogs**: Using a plug with energy monitoring on appliances you're curious about — that old extra refrigerator, the gaming PC, the fish tank heater — reveals actual costs and often produces more energy conservation behavior than any informational campaign.

## 2.4GHz vs. 5GHz WiFi: What You Need to Know

Every smart plug in this guide uses 2.4GHz WiFi, not 5GHz. This is standard for smart home devices and not a defect — 2.4GHz has longer range and better wall/obstacle penetration than 5GHz, which makes it better suited for devices in outlets throughout a home.

The practical implication: if your router broadcasts both 2.4GHz and 5GHz networks with different names, make sure your phone is connected to the 2.4GHz network when setting up a new smart plug. Many routers now use band steering (combining both into one network name), which usually handles this automatically. If you have issues during setup, temporarily connect your phone to the 2.4GHz band manually.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Are smart plugs safe to leave on all the time?**
Yes, from a safety standpoint, smart plugs are tested to UL/CE safety standards and are designed for continuous operation. The plug itself draws a very small amount of standby power (typically 1–2 watts) even when the connected device is off. The same basic safety rules apply as with any electrical device: don't use smart plugs with appliances that exceed their rated amperage (most are rated 15A/1800W), and follow the manufacturer guidelines about what types of appliances are appropriate. Space heaters and large appliances are generally fine on quality smart plugs rated for the load.

**Q: Do smart plugs work if the internet goes down?**
It depends on the plug's architecture. Most smart plugs require internet connectivity for voice assistant commands (Alexa and Google Assistant need cloud connectivity). However, scheduled automations vary: TP-Link Kasa plugs (including the EP25) store schedules locally and continue running them without internet. Wyze and Amazon plugs depend more on cloud connectivity for schedules. Eve Energy (Thread/Matter) processes automations locally via your Apple Home Hub and is notably reliable during internet outages.

**Q: Can smart plugs control high-wattage appliances like air conditioners?**
Standard smart plugs are rated for 15A (1800W) at 120V, which covers most household appliances. However, large window air conditioners (1,000–1,500W) are in the right range. Central HVAC units, electric dryers, ovens, and other high-draw appliances run on 240V circuits and require entirely different solutions — these are not suitable for standard smart plugs. Always verify your appliance's wattage against the plug's rated capacity before connecting.

**Q: Do I need a hub to use a smart plug?**
For Alexa, Google Home, and Samsung SmartThings-compatible plugs: no hub required. You connect via WiFi directly to your router and control via the relevant app or voice assistant. For Apple HomeKit: a Home Hub (HomePod, HomePod mini, or Apple TV 4K) is required for remote access and automations, though basic local control within the home works without one. The Eve Energy (Thread) specifically benefits from a Home Hub for full functionality.

**Q: What's the difference between a smart plug and a smart switch?**
A smart plug replaces nothing — you plug it into an existing outlet and plug your lamp or appliance into it. No wiring, no tools, fully reversible. A smart switch replaces the wall switch in your electrical box, which requires turning off the circuit breaker, removing the old switch, and wiring the new one in. Smart switches control the entire circuit (everything wired to that switch) and are permanent installations. Smart plugs are better for renters, temporary use, or controlling specific devices. Smart switches are better for overhead lighting and any situation where you want to convert a fixed circuit to smart control.
