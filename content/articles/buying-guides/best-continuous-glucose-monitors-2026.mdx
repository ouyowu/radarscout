## What Is a Continuous Glucose Monitor?

A CGM is a small wearable device that measures your blood glucose (blood sugar) levels continuously throughout the day and night — typically every 1–5 minutes — without fingerstick blood draws. A tiny flexible filament called a cannula sits just under the skin (usually on the upper arm or abdomen), measuring glucose in the fluid around your cells. This reading correlates closely with blood glucose, with a slight lag of about 5–10 minutes compared to a direct blood draw.

The sensor connects wirelessly to a receiver device or — in newer systems — directly to your smartphone, displaying a real-time graph of your glucose levels. You can see where your glucose is right now, where it's been over the past 24 hours, and (with some devices) receive alerts when it's rising or falling too fast or crossing dangerous thresholds.

For someone with Type 1 diabetes, this replaces dozens of daily fingerstick tests and provides the kind of continuous visibility that makes insulin dosing far more precise. For people with Type 2 diabetes, the behavioral feedback loop — seeing exactly how a specific meal or activity affects blood sugar — is a powerful tool for lifestyle management. For non-diabetics, the data surfaces patterns that most people would otherwise never see.

### How CGMs Are Different from Blood Glucose Meters

Traditional blood glucose meters (BGMs) give you a point-in-time measurement when you prick your finger and apply blood to a test strip. They're accurate, but they only show you where glucose is at that exact moment. A CGM shows you the direction of travel and rate of change — which is often more actionable. Knowing your glucose is 140 mg/dL tells you where you are. Knowing it's 140 and rising steeply after a meal tells you something different than knowing it's 140 and falling steadily.

The trend arrows and graphs that CGMs provide change how people with diabetes manage insulin and how health-conscious people understand their metabolic responses.

### 1. Dexcom G7 — Best Clinical CGM for Diabetes Management

**Wear time**: 10 days  
**Calibration**: Not required (factory-calibrated)  
**Alerts**: Yes, fully customizable real-time alerts  
**Integration**: Apple Health, Google Fit, Apple Watch, compatible insulin pumps  
**Prescription**: Required  
**Sensor size**: 60% smaller than G6

**Why it leads the clinical category**: The Dexcom G7 represents a significant hardware leap from the G6. The sensor is 60% smaller, the transmitter and sensor are now a single integrated unit (no separate transmitter to reuse), and the warmup time dropped from 2 hours to 30 minutes — practically irrelevant for most days, but meaningful when you're inserting a new sensor at an inconvenient time.

Accuracy is where Dexcom has historically differentiated itself, and the G7 maintains that advantage. The MARD (Mean Absolute Relative Difference) — the standard accuracy metric — for the G7 is 8.2%, which is among the lowest in the market. Lower MARD means the CGM reading deviates less from what a simultaneous laboratory blood draw would show. For insulin dosing decisions, this accuracy differential has real clinical significance.

The alert system is extensively customizable. You can set thresholds for urgent lows, lows, highs, and rate-of-change alerts (rising or falling too quickly). Alert schedules can be set differently for daytime and nighttime. The G7 also integrates with most modern closed-loop insulin delivery systems — the artificial pancreas setups where an insulin pump and CGM communicate automatically to adjust insulin delivery — which is a major clinical consideration for many Type 1 diabetes patients.

Dexcom's Share feature allows designated followers (family members, parents of children with diabetes, caregivers) to receive real-time glucose data on their own phones, enabling remote monitoring that has been genuinely transformative for parents of children with Type 1 diabetes.

## CGMs for Non-Diabetics: The Biohacker Angle

A significant and growing market segment uses CGMs not to manage a diabetes diagnosis but to understand metabolic health more generally. The science behind this application is straightforward: blood glucose fluctuations happen in everyone, diabetic or not, and the amplitude and pattern of those fluctuations correlate with how your body is processing food, stress, sleep deprivation, and exercise.

You might eat two meals with the same calorie count and macros and have dramatically different glucose responses — because of food preparation, individual microbiome differences, meal timing, prior exercise, or sleep quality. Without a CGM, these differences are invisible. With one, you can connect cause and effect in ways that change behavior.

### Why Non-Diabetics Can't Just Buy a CGM

CGMs require a prescription in the United States. They're approved as medical devices for diabetes management, not general wellness. A non-diabetic person cannot walk into a pharmacy and purchase Dexcom or Libre sensors without a valid prescription from a licensed provider.

This is where intermediary programs come in.

### Levels and Nutrisense: Prescription + Platform for Non-Diabetics

Companies like **Levels** and **Nutrisense** have built programs that combine prescription access, sensor supply, and a proprietary software layer on top of the raw CGM data. Here's how they typically work:

1. You complete a health intake form online
2. An affiliated physician reviews your information and issues a CGM prescription (in states where this is permitted via telehealth)
3. Sensors (typically Abbott FreeStyle Libre) are shipped to you directly
4. You wear the sensors and use the company's app rather than (or in addition to) the manufacturer's app
5. The company's software interprets your glucose data in a wellness context — meal scoring, metabolic patterns, activity correlations

**Levels** focuses on deep data visualization and education. The app shows glucose responses to specific meals you log, surfaces patterns across your lifestyle data, and provides a metabolic score. The program attracts the quantified-self crowd, people experimenting with dietary approaches (ketogenic, low-carb, time-restricted eating), and athletes monitoring training recovery. Levels costs approximately $199/month, which includes sensors and platform access.

**Nutrisense** pairs CGM data with access to registered dietitians who provide personalized interpretation and coaching based on your glucose patterns. For people who want human guidance rather than (or in addition to) algorithmic analysis, Nutrisense's dietitian access is a differentiator. Pricing ranges from approximately $179–$225/month depending on plan tier.

Both programs are significantly more expensive than what a diabetic patient pays with insurance coverage — but they solve the prescription access problem and add a software layer that makes the data more actionable for a non-clinical audience.

## Understanding CGM Accuracy: What MARD Actually Means

The accuracy metric that appears in every CGM comparison is MARD — Mean Absolute Relative Difference. It represents, on average, how far a CGM reading deviates from a simultaneous laboratory blood glucose measurement.

A MARD of 8.2% (Dexcom G7) means the CGM reading is, on average, within 8.2% of the true blood glucose value. At a blood glucose of 100 mg/dL, that's an average error of about 8 mg/dL in either direction. At 200 mg/dL (a high post-meal reading), the average error is about 16 mg/dL.

For clinical context: the FDA requires a MARD below 15% for CGM approval. Most modern CGMs are well below that, but the difference between 8.2% (Dexcom G7) and 9.2% (Libre 3) matters more at high or low glucose values — exactly the ranges where dosing decisions are most consequential.

For non-diabetic biohacker use, accuracy differences between Dexcom and Libre are largely irrelevant since you're not making insulin dosing decisions based on readings.

## Insurance Coverage and Prescription Access

**For people with diabetes**: CGM coverage has expanded significantly. Most major commercial insurance plans, Medicare Part B, and Medicaid in most states cover CGMs for qualifying patients. Coverage requirements typically include a diabetes diagnosis and, for Medicare, an intensive insulin treatment regimen. Your endocrinologist or primary care physician should be familiar with the coverage requirements for your plan.

**The coverage gap**: Even with insurance, out-of-pocket costs vary widely based on your specific plan, deductible status, and formulary. Dexcom G7 sensors typically run $250–$450/month without insurance. Libre 3 sensors run $75–$150/month without insurance. With insurance and patient assistance programs (both Abbott and Dexcom have them), costs can drop to near zero for qualifying patients.

**For non-diabetics using biohacker programs**: No insurance coverage. Full subscription cost out of pocket. The programs recognize this and price accordingly — though $179–$200/month for a few months of metabolic insight is a price point many in the health-optimization space accept.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do CGMs hurt to insert or wear?**
The insertion uses a spring-loaded applicator that inserts a small flexible cannula under the skin in under a second. Most people describe it as feeling like a mild pinch, comparable to or less than a fingerstick blood draw. The sensor itself is not felt during wear — the cannula is flexible and sits in subcutaneous fat. Some people experience mild itching from the adhesive, particularly if skin is sensitive; medical-grade adhesive patches can be placed under the sensor to reduce skin contact if this is an issue.

**Q: Can I shower, swim, or exercise while wearing a CGM?**
Yes. Both Dexcom and Libre sensors are waterproof — rated to 8 feet for 24 hours. Showering, bathing, and swimming are all fine. Sweat during intense exercise can occasionally affect adhesion, and an adhesive overpatch (available from both manufacturers and third-party sellers) helps keep the sensor secure during activities that cause heavy sweating. Exercise itself doesn't damage the sensor or significantly affect accuracy.

**Q: How accurate are CGMs compared to fingerstick meters?**
Modern CGMs are highly accurate — within clinical requirements for insulin dosing decisions. The 8–10% MARD of current CGMs is in practice very close to fingerstick meters, which also have their own measurement variability. The main difference is that CGMs measure interstitial fluid glucose, which lags blood glucose by about 5–10 minutes. During periods of rapid glucose change (large meals, intense exercise, insulin action peaks), this lag means CGM readings slightly trail where blood glucose actually is at that exact moment. This is why CGM trend arrows matter as much as point values.

**Q: I don't have diabetes. Will a CGM tell me anything useful?**
For most metabolically healthy people, glucose levels are well-regulated and don't swing dramatically. You may see modest post-meal spikes and learn which foods cause higher responses for you personally — this varies more between individuals than most people realize. The most useful applications for non-diabetics are learning how different foods affect your personal glucose response, understanding the glucose impact of sleep deprivation and stress, and monitoring metabolic health proactively (catching insulin resistance patterns early). Whether this is worth $200/month for a few months of data is a personal value judgment.

**Q: Do I need a reader device or can I use my phone?**
All current Dexcom and Libre sensors work with compatible smartphones as the receiver — no separate reader device is required. Dexcom G7 requires an Android or iPhone running current OS versions. Libre 3 similarly supports a broad range of current smartphones via the LibreLink app. Dedicated receiver devices are still available for purchase and are used by some patients who prefer not to rely on their phone, but for most users, the smartphone app is the primary interface.

## The Bottom Line

For people managing diabetes, the right CGM comes down to a few factors:

Choose the **Dexcom G7** if accuracy is your top priority, you use a closed-loop insulin pump system, or you need extensive alarm customization. It's the clinical leader.

Choose the **FreeStyle Libre 3** if you want the best combination of value, wearability, and sensor life — 14 days is genuinely convenient, the sensor is the smallest available, and the cost difference with insurance coverage is meaningful.

The **FreeStyle Libre 2** remains appropriate for cost-sensitive patients who don't need passive real-time streaming.

The **Dexcom G6** is worth sticking with only if your existing pump integration or insurance situation makes switching to G7 impractical.

For non-diabetics curious about metabolic health, **Levels** is the best choice for data-focused self-experimentation, while **Nutrisense** is the right pick if you want personalized dietitian coaching alongside your glucose data. Neither is cheap — budget for 2–3 months minimum to collect enough data to see meaningful patterns.
