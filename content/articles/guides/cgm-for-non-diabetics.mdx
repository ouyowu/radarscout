Five years ago, wearing a continuous glucose monitor as a healthy person would have gotten you strange looks. Today, athletes, executives, and longevity-focused individuals are among the fastest-growing CGM user segments. The data streaming from a small sensor on your arm is prompting people to rethink what they eat, when they eat, and how sleep and stress silently undermine metabolic health.

This guide explains what CGMs do, why healthy people use them, what the data means, and whether the cost is justified.

---

## What Is a CGM?

A continuous glucose monitor (CGM) is a small wearable sensor — typically applied to the back of the upper arm — that measures blood glucose levels continuously, usually every one to five minutes. Unlike a fingerstick blood glucose meter, which gives you a single snapshot, a CGM gives you a real-time curve of how your glucose rises, peaks, and falls throughout the day and night.

CGMs were developed for people with Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes, who need to know their glucose levels to manage insulin dosing and avoid dangerous highs and lows. The FDA-cleared devices on the market were designed and approved with diabetic patients in mind.

However, the hardware works the same on a healthy person — and increasingly, both the medical community and the wellness industry are exploring what that continuous stream of metabolic data can reveal for people without diabetes.

## Why Non-Diabetics Are Using CGMs

### Metabolic Health

Most people assume that because they're not diabetic, their blood sugar is fine. The reality is more nuanced. A 2018 Stanford study (frequently cited in CGM literature) found that even metabolically healthy individuals experience significant glucose spikes after certain meals — spikes that would be clinically notable if they occurred in a diabetic context.

Pre-diabetes affects an estimated 96 million American adults, the majority of whom are undiagnosed. A CGM worn for two to four weeks can reveal whether your post-meal glucose patterns look metabolically healthy or whether certain foods are pushing you into territory worth addressing.

### Weight Loss and Nutrition

Food tracking apps ask you to log what you eat. CGMs show you what that food actually does to your body. Two people eating identical meals can have dramatically different glucose responses due to gut microbiome composition, sleep status, stress levels, and genetics. A CGM cuts through generic dietary advice and shows you your personal response.

High glycemic variability — large swings between highs and lows — is associated with increased hunger, poor energy levels, and fat storage. Keeping glucose more stable through food choices, meal timing, and movement appears to reduce cravings and improve body composition in some users.

### Athletic Performance

Endurance athletes are using CGMs to refine fueling strategies. Understanding exactly when blood glucose drops during a long run or bike ride allows for more precise carbohydrate timing. Some elite runners have used CGM data to identify that they were under-fueling in training — a surprisingly common problem — and adjusted accordingly with measurable improvements in performance.

Post-workout glucose dips can also reveal over-training signals before conventional metrics like heart rate variability show up.

### Longevity and Healthspan

The longevity community — researchers and practitioners focused on extending healthy lifespan — has become one of the most enthusiastic CGM user groups. Chronic glucose dysregulation and high time above normal glucose ranges are associated with accelerated aging markers, including glycation (the process by which glucose molecules damage proteins throughout the body). Minimizing unnecessary glucose spikes over decades may have compounding benefits for metabolic and cardiovascular health.

Physicians in the longevity space, including those practicing functional or precision medicine, increasingly incorporate CGM data into annual metabolic assessments.

## How CGMs Work

CGMs work by measuring glucose in the interstitial fluid — the fluid surrounding cells in the tissue just beneath the skin — rather than directly in the blood. A tiny flexible filament (about the width of a hair) is inserted just below the skin surface, where an electrochemical reaction produces a signal proportional to glucose concentration.

This signal is transmitted via Bluetooth to a smartphone app, where it's displayed as a real-time graph. Most current CGMs update every one to five minutes and store up to 90 days of historical data.

The slight delay between blood glucose and interstitial fluid glucose (typically 5–15 minutes) means CGMs aren't perfectly synchronized with actual blood values during rapid changes — like the first 20 minutes after a meal — but for trend monitoring and pattern recognition in non-diabetic use cases, this lag is rarely consequential.

Current-generation sensors are worn for 10–14 days before replacement. Applying a new sensor takes about 30 seconds and is nearly painless. The sensors are waterproof and stay in place through exercise, showering, and sleep.

## Best CGMs for Non-Diabetics in 2026

Non-diabetics in the US cannot simply purchase a CGM over the counter — these are FDA-regulated medical devices that require a prescription. Two programs have emerged to bridge this gap for healthy users: **Levels Health** and **Nutrisense**. Both obtain prescriptions on your behalf, ship the CGM hardware, and provide apps and coaching to help interpret the data.

### Dexcom G7 (via Levels Health)

The Dexcom G7 is the most accurate consumer CGM on the market and the preferred hardware for Levels Health. It offers a 10-day wear period, a 30-minute warm-up time (down from the G6's two hours), and a noticeably smaller sensor than previous generations.

Levels overlays the CGM data with a food logging interface, activity data from your wearable, and a metabolic score for each meal. The app flags which meals caused significant glucose spikes and suggests lower-glycemic alternatives. Levels has published peer-reviewed research on continuous glucose monitoring in non-diabetic populations and has a scientific advisory board drawn from academic medicine and metabolism research.

**Best for:** People who want deep nutritional insights and a polished app experience.

### Abbott Freestyle Libre 3 (via Nutrisense)

The Freestyle Libre 3 is Abbott's latest CGM — thinner than a stack of three pennies and the smallest CGM on the market as of 2026. It offers 14-day wear (vs. Dexcom's 10 days), real-time readings, and an integrated alarm system. Nutrisense bundles the Libre 3 with registered dietitian coaching, offering monthly video calls and async message support.

Nutrisense's approach is more coaching-intensive: rather than just showing you data, their dietitians contextualize it, suggest specific interventions, and follow up on outcomes. For users who want human accountability alongside data, this is valuable.

**Best for:** People who want dietitian guidance alongside the CGM data.

## What the Data Actually Tells You

Raw CGM data can be overwhelming without a framework. Here are the key metrics that matter for healthy users:

**Glucose spikes (post-meal peaks).** A post-meal rise of 30–40 mg/dL is typical and benign. Spikes above 140 mg/dL are considered elevated even in non-diabetic individuals, and consistent spikes above 180 mg/dL start to correlate with adverse outcomes in research literature. CGM data shows you which foods — and which meal contexts — push you toward or beyond these thresholds.

**Time in range.** For non-diabetics, "optimal range" is typically defined as 70–140 mg/dL. Levels and Nutrisense both calculate what percentage of your day you spend in this range. Highly metabolically fit individuals often spend 90%+ of their day in range. Seeing this number move in response to dietary changes is one of the most motivating feedback loops CGMs provide.

**Metabolic flexibility.** Healthy glucose regulation includes the ability to return to baseline quickly after a meal — typically within two hours. Slow return-to-baseline (still elevated three or more hours post-meal) is a sign of reduced insulin sensitivity, which is worth addressing regardless of whether you meet diagnostic criteria for pre-diabetes.

**Nocturnal glucose patterns.** Sleep is when your body handles repair and metabolic reset. Elevated glucose during sleep — often caused by a late high-carb dinner, alcohol, or cortisol elevation from poor sleep quality — is a pattern CGMs uniquely reveal. Many users are surprised to find that their worst metabolic hours are not after lunch but between 2 and 5 AM.

**Dawn phenomenon.** Many people experience a natural rise in glucose in the early morning hours (roughly 4–8 AM), driven by cortisol and other counter-regulatory hormones that prepare the body for waking. Seeing this on a CGM for the first time is often a moment of "aha" for users who worried their fasting glucose was diet-related.

## Is It Safe? What Doctors Say

For healthy adults, wearing a CGM carries minimal physical risk. The sensor insertion is superficial and the risk of infection is low when proper application hygiene is followed. The devices are FDA-cleared medical devices with established safety profiles.

The more nuanced concern raised by some physicians is psychological: CGM data can, for some users, trigger anxiety or orthorexia-adjacent food behaviors — an obsessive focus on keeping glucose "perfect" that becomes disruptive. If you have a history of disordered eating or health anxiety, a CGM may amplify those tendencies rather than provide useful insight. Discuss with your doctor before starting.

Most physicians who are comfortable with CGM use in non-diabetic populations recommend wearing it for two to four weeks as a diagnostic tool, learning the patterns, making targeted changes, and then removing it — rather than wearing one indefinitely. Using a CGM as a temporary data-gathering exercise is a reasonable framing.

Some endocrinologists remain cautious about direct-to-consumer CGM programs, noting that interstitial glucose is not blood glucose, that non-diabetic reference ranges are less established in the literature, and that the programs' coaches are not clinicians. These are fair caveats. The field is moving quickly, and the science is solid enough to inform lifestyle decisions even if the clinical norms are still being established.

## Cost: What to Expect

CGM hardware for non-diabetics is not covered by insurance in most cases, which means out-of-pocket costs apply.

**Levels Health:** Approximately $199/month, which includes the CGM sensors (two Dexcom G7 sensors = 20 days of data), the Levels app subscription, and access to the coaching platform. A founding member rate of $149/month has occasionally been available.

**Nutrisense:** Approximately $149–$199/month depending on plan tier. The higher tier includes monthly registered dietitian calls; the lower tier is app-only with async messaging access. Nutrisense uses the Freestyle Libre 3 sensor.

**Standalone hardware (with a prescription from your doctor):** A single Dexcom G7 sensor retails for approximately $35–$45. The Libre 3 sensor runs approximately $40–$50 per 14-day sensor. If your doctor is willing to write a prescription and you're comfortable interpreting the data yourself, the monthly hardware cost is roughly $70–$100.

**Bottom line:** Plan for $100–$200/month all-in. Many users run a single two-to-four week experiment to gather baseline data and establish dietary patterns, then repeat the experiment after making changes. You don't need to wear a CGM continuously to get value from it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Do I need a prescription to get a CGM as a non-diabetic?**
In the US, yes — CGMs are FDA-regulated Class II medical devices that require a prescription. Programs like Levels and Nutrisense handle this by connecting you with a telehealth physician who issues the prescription on your behalf as part of the program sign-up. In some European countries and Canada, CGMs are available over the counter.

**Q: Will a CGM tell me if I'm pre-diabetic?**
A CGM can reveal post-meal glucose patterns consistent with reduced insulin sensitivity, but it cannot diagnose pre-diabetes — that requires a clinical fasting glucose test or HbA1c test ordered by a physician. If your CGM data concerns you, the appropriate next step is to bring it to your doctor and request the standard diagnostic bloodwork.

**Q: Does exercise affect my CGM readings?**
Yes, in multiple ways. Aerobic exercise generally lowers glucose, often noticeably during and after a workout. High-intensity exercise can temporarily raise glucose due to liver glycogen release. Strength training can cause a modest glucose spike. These patterns are normal, and seeing them on a CGM is useful context for fueling and recovery decisions.

**Q: Can I trust CGM accuracy if I don't have diabetes?**
The Dexcom G7 and Freestyle Libre 3 are both FDA-cleared for use in people with diabetes and have mean absolute relative difference (MARD) rates of around 8–9%, meaning they are within about 8–9% of blood glucose values on average. This is accurate enough for trend monitoring and pattern recognition, which is what non-diabetic use cases rely on. For medical decisions — like insulin dosing — a fingerstick confirmation would be needed, but non-diabetic users are not making those decisions.
