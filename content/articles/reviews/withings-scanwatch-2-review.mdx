Most smartwatches have the same problem: they look like smartwatches. The rectangular slabs of glass and silicone broadcasting from your wrist are unmistakably tech — useful at the gym, borderline inappropriate at a board meeting or wedding. Withings has spent years arguing there's a better way. The ScanWatch 2 is their most persuasive argument yet.

At $349, the ScanWatch 2 is a genuine Swiss-made analog watch with real hands, a sapphire crystal, and the kind of restrained elegance that draws "is that a TAG Heuer?" questions. Tucked beneath that analog face is a 40mm case housing medical-grade ECG, SpO2 monitoring, sleep tracking, continuous heart rate, and all the sensors you'd expect from a serious health wearable. The OLED display that lives inside the watch face illuminates on demand for notifications, metrics, and alerts, then disappears — leaving just the analog watch you'd actually want to wear.

This is that rare product that manages to be genuinely good at two different things simultaneously.

## Design and Build Quality

Let's start where most smartwatch reviews end: the wristwatch credentials.

The ScanWatch 2 comes in two case sizes — 38mm and 42mm — and two colorways at launch: black/dark chrome and pearl/rose gold. The case is stainless steel, the crystal is sapphire (scratch-resistant to a degree that most smartwatches with mineral glass simply aren't), and the case back has medical-grade sensors flush-mounted under a curved ceramic plate.

The dial is clean. Dauphine hands sweep over a textured dial, sub-dials at 3 and 9 o'clock display battery level and step count respectively. The OLED display sits just below center — invisible at rest, crisp when illuminated. The watch has no visible buttons except a discreet crown at 3 o'clock.

On the wrist, it sits at 11mm thick — slightly thicker than a pure dress watch but thinner than an Apple Watch Series 9 or Galaxy Watch 6 by a meaningful margin. With the included leather strap or the optional silicone sports band, it wears like a watch, not a gadget.

Water resistance is rated to 30 meters (3 ATM), sufficient for hand washing, rain, and light swimming. Not suitable for lap swimming or water sports.

**Verdict on design:** Withings has genuinely solved the "I don't want to look like I'm wearing a fitness tracker" problem. For professional environments where a traditional smartwatch reads as casual, the ScanWatch 2 reads as a watch — full stop.

## Health Tracking Features

### ECG (Electrocardiogram)

The ScanWatch 2 carries an FDA-cleared ECG function, which places it alongside the Apple Watch Series and Samsung Galaxy Watch in a small club of consumer devices with regulatory clearance for this feature.

To take a reading, you tap the crown with your opposite hand, hold still for 30 seconds, and the watch captures a single-lead ECG. The Health Mate app generates a PDF report you can share with your cardiologist. The primary use case is screening for atrial fibrillation — an irregular heart rhythm that is common, underdiagnosed, and associated with stroke risk.

In testing over several weeks, the ECG function consistently produced readable traces. The app correctly classified sinus rhythm in all normal readings and flagged one test case with deliberate arrhythmia simulation as "inconclusive — consult a physician," which is the appropriate clinical response for edge cases.

One limitation: the ScanWatch 2 takes ECG readings on demand only, not continuously. The Apple Watch can take a background continuous ECG (limited capability) and detect irregular rhythm passively. If continuous background AFib monitoring is a priority, Apple Watch has the edge. For most users who want occasional check-ins and the ability to send an ECG to their doctor, the ScanWatch 2 is more than sufficient.

### SpO2 (Blood Oxygen Saturation)

The ScanWatch 2 measures blood oxygen saturation using the same PPG optical method as competitors. In spot-check mode (on demand), it's accurate within 2% of clinical pulse oximeter readings — acceptable for wellness monitoring, not for clinical oxygen management.

The standout feature here is overnight SpO2 monitoring. The watch automatically tracks blood oxygen throughout the night and flags patterns consistent with sleep-disordered breathing (low oxygen dips associated with sleep apnea). The Health Mate app displays a "Breathing Disturbances" score each morning. This is not a medical diagnosis — if the watch flags concerning patterns, it recommends consulting a physician for a formal sleep study — but as a screening tool for a condition that affects roughly 25% of adults and is dramatically underdiagnosed, it provides real value.

### Sleep Tracking

Sleep tracking is where the ScanWatch 2 earns its money for many users. Withings has over a decade of sleep science behind their algorithms, and the ScanWatch 2's output — detailed light/deep/REM cycle breakdown, sleep score, heart rate and SpO2 overnight, and breathing disturbance indicator — is among the best available on a wrist-worn device.

The sleep score (0–100) synthesizes duration, sleep cycle quality, interruptions, and SpO2 stability. After a few weeks, the app builds a baseline and shows trends — a genuinely useful feature for identifying whether lifestyle changes (earlier dinner, no alcohol, earlier bedtime) are having measurable effects on sleep quality.

Compared to Oura Ring 4 — the current benchmark for wrist-adjacent sleep tracking — the ScanWatch 2 holds up well on sleep stage detection and SpO2 overnight monitoring. The Oura Ring still has an edge for skin temperature tracking and longer historical trend analysis, but the gap has narrowed meaningfully with the ScanWatch 2 generation.

### Activity and Fitness Tracking

The ScanWatch 2 tracks steps, distance, calories burned, active minutes, and automatic workout detection for 45 activity types including running, swimming, cycling, yoga, and strength training. GPS is available via connected GPS (using your phone's GPS) — there is no standalone GPS onboard, which is the most significant functional trade-off versus a dedicated fitness watch.

For casual exercisers, connected GPS is generally adequate. For runners who want pace-per-mile data without carrying a phone, it's a meaningful limitation. If GPS tracking is a priority, the Garmin Forerunner 265 or Apple Watch Ultra 2 are better options.

Heart rate monitoring is continuous and accurate during steady-state cardio. Like most optical HR sensors, accuracy drops during high-intensity intervals with significant wrist motion. For serious athletes, a chest strap (Polar H10) paired via Bluetooth provides better data during hard workouts.

### Additional Health Features

- **Continuous heart rate monitoring** with high/low alerts (configurable thresholds)
- **VO2 Max estimate** derived from heart rate and activity data
- **Stress monitoring** via heart rate variability analysis
- **Women's health / cycle tracking** integrated with Health Mate and Apple Health
- **Skin temperature deviation** tracking during sleep (new in ScanWatch 2)

## Battery Life

The 30-day battery life in "watch mode" is the ScanWatch 2's single most compelling feature for many users. In practice, with all health tracking features enabled (continuous HR, sleep tracking, SpO2 monitoring, SpO2 breathing disturbance scan), expect 20–25 days between charges. With smartwatch features like constant notification delivery and frequent OLED wake, expect 2–3 days.

Most users land somewhere in the middle — notifications on, health tracking on, OLED display on-demand — and report 10–18 days between charges. This is still dramatically better than any full smartwatch on the market.

The watch charges via Withings' proprietary magnetic puck (USB-A). A full charge takes approximately 90 minutes. The proprietary connector is a minor annoyance — one more cable to pack — but the low charging frequency makes it less irritating in practice than it sounds.

For context: Apple Watch Series 9 lasts ~18 hours, Samsung Galaxy Watch 6 ~40 hours, Garmin Fenix 7 in smartwatch mode ~18 days (GPS off). The ScanWatch 2's battery life in full-feature mode is genuinely competitive even against GPS-focused watches.

## Software: Withings Health Mate

The Health Mate app (iOS and Android) is Withings' hub for all their health devices, and it shows — the depth of data available is impressive. The app displays timeline views of sleep, activity, heart rate, SpO2, and ECG data with configurable date ranges. The "Health+" section synthesizes data into trend reports.

A $9.95/month Health+ subscription unlocks advanced insights, cardiovascular health age estimation, and detailed sleep coaching. The core features — data viewing, ECG reading, sleep tracking, and activity tracking — are available without a subscription. This compares favorably to Oura's required subscription for any meaningful insight.

The app is not as polished as Apple Health or Garmin Connect for athletes, but for the health monitoring use case the ScanWatch 2 is designed for, it's more than adequate. Apple Health and Google Fit integration both work well.

Notification support covers calls, texts, and app notifications. Reply is not supported — this is a watch, not a wrist-mounted phone. You'll see the notification content and can dismiss it; that's the extent of notification interaction.

## Compatibility

- **iOS:** Full support. Syncs with Apple Health. ECG reports generated and stored in Health Mate.
- **Android:** Full support. Syncs with Google Fit. All health features available.
- **No proprietary ecosystem lock:** Unlike Apple Watch (iOS only) or Samsung Galaxy Watch (Wear OS optimized for Samsung), the ScanWatch 2 works equally well on both platforms.

## Who Should Buy the Withings ScanWatch 2

**Buy it if you:**
- Want a watch that looks like a watch but tracks your health seriously
- Value ECG and overnight SpO2 monitoring as health screening tools
- Find the daily charging requirement of Apple Watch or Samsung Galaxy Watch disruptive
- Want a single watch for professional environments and gym sessions
- Are an iPhone and Android agnostic household

**Skip it if you:**
- Run or cycle seriously and want standalone GPS
- Want LTE/cellular for leaving your phone behind
- Need voice assistant integration (Siri, Google Assistant)
- Want real-time workout coaching or navigation features
- Prioritize the widest app ecosystem

## Verdict

The Withings ScanWatch 2 occupies a specific and underserved niche: health-serious adults who want medical-grade monitoring, days-long battery, and a watch they can wear to work without looking like they're about to run a 5K.

At $349, it is not cheap — but it is well made, FDA-cleared for ECG, and backed by a software platform with real depth. The lack of standalone GPS and LTE are real limitations if you want a fitness-first device. For everyone else — and there are more of "everyone else" than the smartwatch industry sometimes acknowledges — the ScanWatch 2 is the most complete answer available.

**Rating: 4.4 / 5**
